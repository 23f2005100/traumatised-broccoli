# The Traumatised Broccoli — What Children Are Given To Grow In

## About This Work

This image was made for TDS Project1 : The Affective Chart.
But it was also made from something real.

Growing up in an environment shaped by abuse — emotional, 
physical, psychological — teaches you a specific lesson 
that nobody says out loud: that the warmth other people 
take for granted was never yours to begin with. 
You watch the world go on normally.

You see other children with things you didn't have —
not just objects, but safety. Stability. The freedom
to just be a child. And somewhere inside, you start
to believe that the difference is something you did.
That you deserved this. That if you had been different,
better, quieter, less of a problem — maybe the soil
under your feet would have been different too.

You compare yourself constantly. Not to measure how far
you've come, but to measure how far behind you are.
You see the life you could have had — the version of you
that grew up in warmth instead of cold — and you grieve
for her. Even though she never existed. Even though you're
the one who survived.

The question becomes: what did I do to deserve this?
And the terrible answer is: nothing. You did nothing.
The soil was broken before you were planted in it.

This image tries to show what that looks like.

This image tries to give it one.

## The Dataset

**CDC BRFSS Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACE) Module**  
https://www.cdc.gov/violenceprevention/aces/ace-brfss.html

Adverse Childhood Experiences are defined as potentially 
traumatic events occurring before the age of 18 — including 
physical, emotional and sexual abuse; neglect; witnessing 
domestic violence; household substance abuse; mental illness 
in the home; incarceration of a family member; and divorce.

### What The Data Says

- **2 in 3 adults** have experienced at least one ACE
- **1 in 6 adults** have experienced four or more ACEs
- Children with 4+ ACEs are **12 times more likely** 
  to attempt suicide as adults
- ACEs are linked to **21 million cases of depression**,
  1.9 million cases of heart disease, and shortened 
  life expectancy
- The effects do not end when the childhood does — 
  they compound silently across an entire lifetime

The data does not measure what it felt like to be that 
child. It only measures what happened afterward, 
decades later, in bodies and lives.

---

## The Insight

> Two in three children are given damaged soil to grow in.  
> The ones who survive are not stronger.  
> They were just slightly luckier with where they were planted.

## The Image

Nine patches of soil. Six cracked, grey, depleted.  
Three dark, moist, alive.

In each damaged patch — something a child left behind.  
A torn family photograph. A shoe worn through the sole.  
A shirt mended so many times the patches have patches.  
A cracked phone screen — the only window to the outside 
world, broken. Knotted earphones — the only private world, 
tangled and buried. A SIM card snapped in two — 
the last connection, severed.

In each damaged patch — a sapling that was planted 
at the same time as the others. Given the same amount 
of time to grow. But the soil was never right.  
Some are bent. Some barely broke the surface.  
One is just a dry twig with no leaves left.

One sapling is bent sideways, still faintly green,  
leaning toward the warmth from the patch next to it.  
It has not made it yet. But it has not stopped.

The three healthy patches have no objects buried in them.  
Just soil. Just light. Just the ordinary conditions  
that should have been ordinary for everyone.

---

## The Visual Argument

A viewer who has never seen the dataset should be able 
to count the ratio — six to three, two out of every three — 
without being told. The fact is in the ground. 
The feeling is in what was buried there.

No labels. No axes. No legend.  
The soil is the data.  
The objects are the lives.  
The ratio is the truth.
